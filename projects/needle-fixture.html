Automated Needle-Handling / Puncture Fixture

Non-proprietary mechanical design case study focused on improving repeatability, handling, and fixture logic for puncture-style testing workflows.

Mechanism design CAD 3D printed prototypes Design for testing

Overview

This project explored fixture concepts for repetitive puncture testing that required many needle exchanges, controlled positioning, and consistent engagement. The work focused on mechanism architecture, CAD iteration, prototype reasoning, and fixture validation thinking without disclosing proprietary design details.

Problem

Manual needle handling can introduce inconsistency, operator burden, and setup time during repetitive test workflows. A better fixture concept needed to support repeatable engagement, controlled indexing, and practical needle retention.

My Role

I designed and iterated non-proprietary fixture concepts, reasoned through mechanism layouts, considered shaft/coupling and motor choices, and used 3D printed prototypes to evaluate physical clearances, indexing behavior, needle retention, and assembly access.

Design / Engineering Approach

  • Explored turret-style mechanisms for indexing multiple needles through a repeatable engagement path with consistent angular spacing.
  • Compared snap-fit needle retention, cartridge/feed concepts, and fixture alignment strategies to reduce manual handling variability.
  • Considered stepper motor integration, shaft support, coupling selection, and repeatable motion constraints.
  • Used prototype iteration to reason through tolerance stackups, clearances, needle access, and assembly accessibility.

Selected Technical Decisions

  • Turret indexing vs. linear feed: a rotary architecture offered compact needle selection, while feed concepts were easier to load but more sensitive to alignment.
  • Snap-fit retention vs. clamping: snap features simplified needle exchange, while clamps offered higher holding force at the cost of added parts and setup time.
  • Stepper-driven indexing vs. manual indexing: motorized motion could improve repeatability, but early hand-actuated prototypes made clearance issues easier to find quickly.
  • 3D printed iteration vs. machined hardware: printed parts accelerated learning before committing to tighter-tolerance components.

What This Demonstrates to Employers

This project demonstrates mechanism design, fixture development, tolerance reasoning, and prototype iteration for medical-device test workflows.

Outcome / What I Learned

The concept work strengthened my approach to fixture architecture, prototype-driven iteration, and mechanical validation planning. I learned how quickly small retention, clearance, and alignment decisions affect the reliability of a test fixture.
