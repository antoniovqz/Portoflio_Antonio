Automated Needle-Handling / Puncture Fixture

Non-proprietary mechanical design case study focused on improving repeatability, handling, and fixture logic for puncture-style testing workflows.

Mechanism design CAD 3D printed prototypes Design for testing

Overview

This project explored fixture concepts for repetitive puncture testing that required many needle exchanges and consistent positioning. The work focused on concept development, CAD iteration, prototype reasoning, and mechanical validation thinking without disclosing proprietary design details.

Problem

Manual needle handling can introduce inconsistency, operator burden, and setup time during repetitive test workflows. A better fixture concept needed to support repeatable engagement, controlled indexing, and practical needle retention.

My Role

I designed and iterated non-proprietary fixture concepts, reasoned through mechanism layouts, considered shaft/coupling and motor choices, and used 3D printed prototypes to evaluate physical clearances and retention approaches.

Design / Engineering Approach

  • Explored turret-style mechanisms for indexing multiple needles through a repeatable engagement path.
  • Compared snap-fit needle retention, cartridge/feed concepts, and fixture alignment strategies.
  • Considered stepper motor integration, shaft support, coupling selection, and repeatable motion constraints.
  • Used prototype iteration to reason through tolerance stackups, clearances, and assembly accessibility.

Key Technical Decisions

  • Prioritized repeatable needle location and user-safe handling over unnecessary complexity.
  • Separated indexing, retention, and engagement functions so each could be evaluated independently.
  • Kept early prototypes 3D-printable to accelerate design learning before committing to machined components.

Outcome / What I Learned

The concept work strengthened my approach to fixture architecture, prototype-driven iteration, and mechanical validation planning. I learned how quickly small retention, clearance, and alignment decisions affect the reliability of a test fixture.
